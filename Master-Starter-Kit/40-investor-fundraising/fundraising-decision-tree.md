# Fundraising Decision Tree

> A structured 5-node decision framework that walks founders through the critical questions before raising capital: whether to raise, what stage you are in, which capital type fits, how much to target, and which investors to pursue.

---

## How to Use This Tree

Start at Node 1 and work through each node sequentially. Each node presents options with pros/cons analysis. Document your decision at each node before moving to the next. Your choices cascade — the decision at Node 1 shapes what is relevant in Nodes 2-5.

---

## Node 1 — Should You Raise External Capital?

This is the most important question. Raising capital is not a default — it is a strategic choice with permanent consequences (dilution, governance obligations, growth expectations, and loss of full control).

### Diagnostic Questions

Before choosing an option, answer these honestly:

- [ ] Can we reach profitability (or next-milestone revenue) with current cash?
- [ ] Is the market opportunity time-sensitive enough that slower growth means losing it?
- [ ] Do we have product-market fit signals, or are we still searching?
- [ ] Are there non-dilutive funding options we have not explored?
- [ ] Do all founders agree on the implications of external capital?
- [ ] Are we raising because we need capital, or because we think we should?

### Option A — Bootstrap (Self-Fund)

**Choose when:** Revenue can fund growth; market is not winner-take-all; founders want full control.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| 100% ownership retained | Slower growth trajectory |
| No board obligations or reporting requirements | Personal financial risk if self-funding |
| Full strategic control | May miss market window in competitive spaces |
| Forces capital efficiency and discipline | Harder to attract top talent without equity war chest |
| No fundraising distraction (3-6 months saved) | Limited ability to invest in R&D or marketing ahead of revenue |

**Best for:** SaaS with early revenue, consulting-to-product transitions, lifestyle businesses with growth ambition, markets where capital efficiency is a competitive advantage.

### Option B — Raise Venture Capital

**Choose when:** Large addressable market (>$1B TAM); product requires significant upfront investment; market is winner-take-all; team can execute at venture-scale velocity.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Capital to invest ahead of revenue | Permanent dilution (typically 15-25% per round) |
| Investor expertise and network | Board governance obligations |
| Signal / credibility for hiring and partnerships | Misaligned incentives if VC timeline differs from founder vision |
| Follow-on capital for subsequent rounds | Fundraising process consumes 3-6 months of founder time |
| Enables aggressive go-to-market spend | Pressure to grow at all costs can destroy unit economics |

**Best for:** Deep tech, marketplaces, platform businesses, companies targeting $100M+ ARR within 7-10 years.

### Option C — Revenue-Based Financing

**Choose when:** Predictable recurring revenue exists; capital needed for specific growth initiatives; founders want to avoid dilution.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| No dilution | Requires existing revenue (typically $50K+ MRR) |
| Flexible repayment tied to revenue | Repayment reduces cash flow during growth phase |
| Faster process than equity raises (days vs months) | Total cost of capital can exceed equity in high-growth scenarios |
| No board seats or governance obligations | Limited capital amounts (typically 3-6x MRR) |
| Founder retains full control | Not suitable for pre-revenue companies |

**Providers to evaluate:** Pipe, Clearco, Capchase, Lighter Capital, Stripe Capital.

**Best for:** SaaS companies with $50K-$500K MRR, e-commerce with predictable revenue, companies needing bridge capital between equity rounds.

### Option D — Grants & Non-Dilutive Funding

**Choose when:** Product has research/innovation component; operating in a sector with government funding (defense, health, climate, education); willing to navigate application bureaucracy.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Zero dilution | Highly competitive application process |
| No repayment obligations | Long timelines (3-12 months from application to funding) |
| Validates technology/market thesis externally | Restrictive use-of-funds requirements |
| Can be combined with other capital sources | Reporting and compliance obligations |
| Some grants provide credibility (e.g., NSF, DARPA) | Amounts may be insufficient as sole funding source |

**Sources to investigate:** SBIR/STTR, NSF I-Corps, state economic development grants, foundation grants (for social impact), EU Horizon (if applicable), industry-specific programs.

**Best for:** Deep tech, biotech, climate tech, education tech, defense tech, social enterprise.

## Node 2 — What Stage Are You?

If you chose "Raise VC" or need to understand your position for revenue-based financing terms, identify your stage. Stage determines valuation expectations, investor types, round sizes, and what you need to prove.

### Diagnostic Questions

- [ ] Do we have a working product or just a concept/prototype?
- [ ] Do we have paying customers? How many?
- [ ] What is our MRR/ARR?
- [ ] Have we demonstrated product-market fit (retention, NPS, organic growth)?
- [ ] Do we have a repeatable sales motion?

### Option A — Pre-Seed

**Typical profile:** Idea stage to early prototype. No or minimal revenue. Team is 1-3 people.

| Parameter | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Round Size | $100K - $1M |
| Valuation | $2M - $6M pre-money |
| Instrument | SAFE (almost always) |
| Key Investors | Angels, pre-seed funds, accelerators |
| What You Need to Show | Team credibility, market insight, early prototype, customer discovery evidence |
| Timeline to Close | 2-8 weeks (for SAFEs) |

### Option B — Seed

**Typical profile:** Working product, early customers (10-100), some revenue ($0-$50K MRR). Team is 3-10 people.

| Parameter | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Round Size | $1M - $4M |
| Valuation | $6M - $20M pre-money |
| Instrument | SAFE or priced round |
| Key Investors | Seed funds, micro VCs, super angels |
| What You Need to Show | Product-market fit signals, early retention data, clear go-to-market hypothesis, capital-efficient growth |
| Timeline to Close | 4-12 weeks |

### Option C — Series A

**Typical profile:** Product-market fit demonstrated. $50K-$300K MRR. Repeatable acquisition channel identified. Team is 10-30 people.

| Parameter | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Round Size | $5M - $20M |
| Valuation | $20M - $80M pre-money |
| Instrument | Priced round (Series A Preferred) |
| Key Investors | Institutional VCs |
| What You Need to Show | Strong retention/engagement metrics, unit economics, scalable GTM motion, clear path to $100M ARR |
| Timeline to Close | 8-16 weeks |

### Option D — Growth (Series B+)

**Typical profile:** Scaling proven model. $300K+ MRR. Multiple acquisition channels. Team is 30-100+ people.

| Parameter | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Round Size | $20M - $100M+ |
| Valuation | $80M - $500M+ pre-money |
| Instrument | Priced round (Series B+ Preferred) |
| Key Investors | Growth-stage VCs, crossover funds, corporate venture |
| What You Need to Show | Revenue growth rate, market leadership evidence, path to profitability, expansion revenue, strong management team |
| Timeline to Close | 12-20 weeks |

## Node 3 — Capital Type

What instrument will you use to raise? This decision has significant implications for cap table complexity, investor rights, and conversion terms.

### Option A — Equity (Priced Round)

**Best for:** Series A and beyond; when you want clean cap table; when valuation is defensible.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Clear valuation and ownership percentages | Legal costs ($20K-$50K+) |
| Investor rights are explicitly negotiated | Longer closing timeline |
| Clean cap table — no conversion ambiguity | Requires board seat allocation |
| Standard institutional VC expectation for Series A+ | Valuation negotiation can stall deals |

### Option B — SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)

**Best for:** Pre-seed and seed; when speed matters; when valuation is hard to determine.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Fast closing (days, not weeks) | Cap table ambiguity until conversion |
| Minimal legal costs ($0-$5K) | Multiple SAFEs with different caps create complexity |
| No board seats or governance requirements | Founders often misunderstand dilution impact |
| Standardized documents (YC SAFE) | Some investors refuse SAFEs at seed stage |

**Key terms to understand:**
- **Valuation Cap:** Maximum valuation at which the SAFE converts to equity
- **Discount Rate:** Percentage discount to the next round's price (typically 15-25%)
- **MFN (Most Favored Nation):** Ensures early SAFE holders get the best terms issued to any SAFE holder
- **Pro-Rata Rights:** Right to invest in future rounds to maintain ownership percentage

### Option C — Convertible Debt (Convertible Note)

**Best for:** Bridge rounds; when debt treatment has tax advantages; investor preference.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Familiar to traditional investors | Interest accrues (increases conversion amount) |
| Maturity date creates urgency to raise next round | Maturity date creates pressure/risk if next round is delayed |
| Can include conversion discount and/or cap | More complex than SAFE |
| Debt on balance sheet (not equity) until conversion | Legal costs higher than SAFE ($5K-$15K) |

### Option D — Revenue-Based Financing

**Best for:** Post-revenue companies; growth capital without dilution; bridge between rounds.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Non-dilutive | Requires existing revenue |
| Fast process | Total repayment exceeds principal (1.3x-2x) |
| No governance implications | Reduces monthly cash flow |
| Flexible repayment | Limited amounts available |

## Node 4 — How Much Should You Raise?

The amount you raise determines your dilution, your runway, and the expectations placed on you. Raise too little and you run out before hitting milestones. Raise too much and you over-dilute, create unrealistic expectations, and may develop capital-inefficient habits.

### The Runway Framework

Calculate your target raise based on monthly burn rate and desired runway:

| Runway Target | Formula | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| 12 months | Monthly Burn x 12 x 1.25 (buffer) | Minimum viable — only if next milestone is close |
| 18 months | Monthly Burn x 18 x 1.25 (buffer) | Standard — enough to hit milestones and begin next raise |
| 24 months | Monthly Burn x 24 x 1.25 (buffer) | Comfortable — recommended for first-time founders |

**The 1.25x buffer:** Things always take longer and cost more than planned. Build a 25% buffer into your raise target.

### Dilution Reality Check

| Round | Typical Dilution | Founder Ownership After (2 founders, equal split) |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-Seed | 5-10% | 45-47.5% each |
| Seed | 15-25% | 34-40% each |
| Series A | 15-25% | 25-34% each |
| Series B | 10-20% | 20-30% each |
| Cumulative through Series B | 45-65% | 17.5-27.5% each |

**Critical insight:** Every dollar you raise has a dilution cost. If you can reach the same milestones with $1.5M instead of $3M, you keep twice as much ownership. But under-raising and running out of cash is far worse than over-dilution.

### Milestone-Based Sizing

Rather than arbitrary amounts, size your round to the milestones you need to hit for the next round:

| Current Stage | Milestones for Next Stage | Typical Capital Required |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-Seed → Seed | MVP, first 10-50 customers, early revenue, PMF signals | $250K - $1M |
| Seed → Series A | $50K+ MRR, proven retention, repeatable GTM, team of 10+ | $1M - $4M |
| Series A → Series B | $200K+ MRR, multiple growth channels, unit economics proven, team of 30+ | $5M - $20M |

## Node 5 — Investor Type

Not all money is equal. The type of investor you pursue affects your fundraising process, timeline, governance, and the value you receive beyond capital.

### Option A — Angel Investors

**Profile:** High-net-worth individuals investing personal capital. Check sizes $10K-$250K.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Fast decisions (days, not months) | Smaller check sizes — need many to fill a round |
| Often domain experts with relevant networks | Variable sophistication — some add noise, not value |
| Less governance overhead | No follow-on capital for later rounds |
| Can be strong references for institutional rounds | Managing 20+ angels is operationally burdensome |

**Best approach:** Warm introductions from founders they have backed. Angel groups and syndicates for efficiency.

### Option B — Micro VCs (Sub-$100M Fund)

**Profile:** Small funds ($10M-$100M AUM) investing at pre-seed and seed. Check sizes $100K-$1M.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Fast decisions relative to institutional VCs | Smaller reserves for follow-on |
| Partners are often former founders | Less brand recognition than top-tier VCs |
| More hands-on support (smaller portfolio) | May not lead later rounds |
| Comfortable with SAFEs and early-stage risk | Some have limited LP networks for introductions |

**Best approach:** Twitter/X presence, warm introductions from portfolio founders, direct cold outreach with strong traction data.

### Option C — Institutional VCs ($100M+ Fund)

**Profile:** Large funds with institutional LPs. Check sizes $1M-$50M+.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Large check sizes — one investor can fill a round | Longer decision process (4-12 weeks) |
| Strong brand signal for hiring and BD | Board seat requirement |
| Deep networks for follow-on funding, hiring, partnerships | Governance overhead and reporting obligations |
| Reserves for pro-rata in later rounds | High bar — fund 1-2% of companies they meet |

**Best approach:** Warm introductions from portfolio founders or other VCs. Cold outreach rarely works. Build relationships 6-12 months before raising.

### Option D — Strategic Investors (Corporate Venture)

**Profile:** Investment arms of large corporations. Check sizes $500K-$10M+.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Domain expertise and market access | May restrict partnerships with competitors |
| Potential customer/distribution relationship | Slower decision process (corporate bureaucracy) |
| Credibility signal in the vertical | Strategic interest may not align with founder vision |
| Sometimes less price-sensitive | Acquisition intent can complicate future fundraising |

**Best approach:** Identify corporations whose strategic roadmap aligns with your product. Engage through business development relationships first.

### Option E — Crowdfunding (Regulation CF / Regulation A+)

**Profile:** Raise from the general public through platforms like Republic, Wefunder, StartEngine.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Builds community of invested supporters | Significant marketing effort required |
| No single investor has control | Large number of shareholders on cap table |
| Public validation of concept | Regulatory compliance costs |
| Can run parallel to other fundraising | Time-intensive campaign management |

**Best approach:** Strong consumer brand, existing community, compelling narrative. Works best as complement to lead investor, not sole source.
