## 1. Origin Story

Your origin story answers the question every potential customer asks: "Why does this exist, and why should I trust the people behind it?" A great origin story makes your product feel inevitable, not accidental.

### Origin Story Template

**Fill in each section, then weave them into a narrative below.**

#### The World Before

> What was the world like before {{PROJECT_NAME}} existed? Describe the landscape, the frustrations, the status quo that everyone accepted as normal.

{{WORLD_BEFORE}}

#### The Personal Pain

> What specific experience did the founder(s) have that made this problem personal? This is not a market analysis -- it is a moment. A specific day, a specific frustration, a breaking point.

{{PERSONAL_PAIN}}

#### The "Aha" Moment

> What was the insight that changed everything? When did the founder realize "this could be done differently"? Be specific about the moment and the realization.

{{AHA_MOMENT}}

#### The First Attempt

> What was the first version? How scrappy was it? What did the earliest users say? Showing vulnerability and humble beginnings makes the story relatable.

{{FIRST_ATTEMPT}}

#### The Mission

> Why does {{PROJECT_NAME}} exist beyond making money? What change do you want to see in the world? What drives the team to keep building?

{{MISSION_STATEMENT}}

#### The Vision

> Where is this going? What does the future look like if {{PROJECT_NAME}} succeeds? Paint a picture of the world you are building toward.

{{VISION_STATEMENT}}

### Assembled Origin Story

Use this structure to write the full narrative. Aim for 300-500 words.

```
[HOOK -- Start with a specific moment, not a summary]

In {{ORIGIN_YEAR}}, {{FOUNDER_NAME}} was {{FOUNDER_SITUATION}}.

Like most {{TARGET_AUDIENCE}}, they {{COMMON_BEHAVIOR}}. But one day,
{{TRIGGERING_EVENT}}.

That's when they realized: {{KEY_INSIGHT}}.

{{FOUNDER_NAME}} started {{FIRST_ACTION}} -- not because they wanted to build
a company, but because they needed a solution that didn't exist.

The first version was {{FIRST_VERSION_DESCRIPTION}}. But even in its simplest
form, {{EARLY_RESULT}}.

Word spread. {{EARLY_TRACTION_STORY}}.

Today, {{PROJECT_NAME}} {{CURRENT_STATE}}. But the mission hasn't changed:
{{MISSION_STATEMENT}}.

We believe {{BELIEF_STATEMENT}}. And we're building {{PROJECT_NAME}} to
make that belief a reality.
```

### Origin Story Variations by Length

| Format | Length | Key Elements to Include | Use For |
|--------|--------|------------------------|---------|
| **Tweet** | 280 chars | Aha moment + mission | Social media bio, tweet thread opener |
| **Elevator** | 30 seconds | Pain + insight + what we built | Networking, podcast intros |
| **About page** | 300-500 words | Full narrative | Website, press kit |
| **Keynote** | 3-5 minutes | Full narrative + visuals + emotion | Conference talks, investor pitches |
| **Blog post** | 800-1200 words | Full narrative + lessons learned | Launch announcement, anniversary post |

### Origin Story Anti-Patterns (What to Avoid)

- **The humble brag:** "We never planned to build a billion-dollar company..." -- audiences see through this
- **The jargon dump:** "We leveraged AI and blockchain to disrupt..." -- tell a human story, not a buzzword story
- **The feature list disguised as a story:** If your origin story sounds like a product demo, start over
- **The false modesty:** Do not pretend you stumbled into success. Own your intention and effort.
- **The competitor attack:** Your origin story is about YOUR journey, not about what competitors do wrong

## 2. Customer Transformation Story Framework

The most powerful marketing stories are not about your product -- they are about your customer's transformation. Your customer is the hero. Your product is the guide.

### The Transformation Arc

```
HERO (Customer) ──> PROBLEM ──> GUIDE (Your Product) ──> PLAN ──> ACTION ──> SUCCESS ──> TRANSFORMATION
     |                |              |                     |         |           |              |
  Who they are    What blocks    How you help      The steps   They act    They win    Who they become
                   them                            to take
```

### Transformation Story Template

#### 1. The Hero (Your Customer)

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Who are they?** | {{HERO_IDENTITY}} |
| **What do they want?** | {{HERO_DESIRE}} |
| **What defines them?** | {{HERO_CHARACTERISTICS}} |
| **Why should the audience relate?** | {{HERO_RELATABILITY}} |

#### 2. The Problem

| Problem Type | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **External problem** (tangible, visible) | {{EXTERNAL_PROBLEM}} |
| **Internal problem** (feeling, frustration) | {{INTERNAL_PROBLEM}} |
| **Philosophical problem** (what's unjust about this) | {{PHILOSOPHICAL_PROBLEM}} |

**Why all three matter:**
- The external problem gets their attention ("I spend 3 hours a day on reports")
- The internal problem drives the purchase ("I feel like I'm wasting my potential")
- The philosophical problem creates brand loyalty ("It shouldn't be this hard to do good work")

#### 3. The Guide ({{PROJECT_NAME}})

The guide is not the hero. The guide has two key traits:

| Trait | How {{PROJECT_NAME}} Demonstrates It |
|-------|--------------------------------------|
| **Empathy** ("We understand your problem") | {{GUIDE_EMPATHY}} |
| **Authority** ("We can actually solve it") | {{GUIDE_AUTHORITY}} |

**Empathy statements:** "We know what it's like to {{SHARED_EXPERIENCE}}."
**Authority signals:** {{AUTHORITY_SIGNALS}} (credentials, customer count, results, awards)

#### 4. The Plan

Give the hero a clear, simple plan. Three steps is ideal.

| Step | Action | What It Accomplishes |
|------|--------|---------------------|
| Step 1 | {{PLAN_STEP_1}} | {{STEP_1_OUTCOME}} |
| Step 2 | {{PLAN_STEP_2}} | {{STEP_2_OUTCOME}} |
| Step 3 | {{PLAN_STEP_3}} | {{STEP_3_OUTCOME}} |

#### 5. The Call to Action

| CTA Type | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Direct** | Tell them exactly what to do | "{{DIRECT_CTA}}" |
| **Transitional** | Offer a lower-commitment step | "{{TRANSITIONAL_CTA}}" |

#### 6. Success (What They Gain)

| Success Element | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Tangible result** | {{SUCCESS_TANGIBLE}} |
| **Emotional result** | {{SUCCESS_EMOTIONAL}} |
| **Status result** | {{SUCCESS_STATUS}} |

#### 7. Failure (What They Avoid)

Be clear about the stakes. What happens if they do NOT act?

| Failure Element | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Tangible loss** | {{FAILURE_TANGIBLE}} |
| **Emotional cost** | {{FAILURE_EMOTIONAL}} |
| **Opportunity cost** | {{FAILURE_OPPORTUNITY}} |

**Note:** Use failure sparingly and ethically. The goal is clarity about stakes, not fear-mongering.

#### 8. The Transformation

| Dimension | Before (Old Identity) | After (New Identity) |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **What they do** | {{TRANSFORM_DO_BEFORE}} | {{TRANSFORM_DO_AFTER}} |

| **What they are** | {{TRANSFORM_ARE_BEFORE}} | {{TRANSFORM_ARE_AFTER}} |

## 4. Before/After Story Template

Before/after stories are the simplest and most effective story format. They work everywhere: landing pages, ads, case studies, social media, email, and sales calls.

### Simple Before/After Template

```
BEFORE {{PROJECT_NAME}}:
{{BEFORE_PARAGRAPH}}

AFTER {{PROJECT_NAME}}:
{{AFTER_PARAGRAPH}}
```

### Detailed Before/After Template

| Dimension | Before | After |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Morning routine** | {{BEFORE_MORNING}} | {{AFTER_MORNING}} |
| **Daily workflow** | {{BEFORE_WORKFLOW}} | {{AFTER_WORKFLOW}} |
| **Biggest frustration** | {{BEFORE_FRUSTRATION}} | {{AFTER_FRUSTRATION}} |
| **End of day feeling** | {{BEFORE_EOD_FEELING}} | {{AFTER_EOD_FEELING}} |
| **Key metric** | {{BEFORE_METRIC}} | {{AFTER_METRIC}} |
| **Team dynamic** | {{BEFORE_TEAM}} | {{AFTER_TEAM}} |
| **Confidence level** | {{BEFORE_CONFIDENCE}} | {{AFTER_CONFIDENCE}} |

### Before/After Writing Tips

1. **Be specific about the "before"** -- vague pain is not compelling. "Spent 3 hours every Monday manually updating the report" beats "Wasted time on reports."
2. **Make the "after" vivid and sensory** -- "Now she opens her laptop Monday morning, sees the dashboard already updated, and dives straight into strategy" beats "Now reports are automated."
3. **Include emotional contrast** -- the emotional shift is more powerful than the functional one. "From dread to excitement" matters more than "from 3 hours to 3 minutes."
4. **Use the customer's own language** -- pull exact quotes from interviews, support tickets, and reviews. Authentic language is more persuasive than polished copywriting.
5. **Anchor the "after" to a specific moment** -- instead of abstract improvement, describe a specific scene: "Last Tuesday, Sarah closed her laptop at 5pm for the first time in months."

### Day-in-the-Life Writing Guide

**The key contrasts to highlight:**

| Contrast | Without Product | With Product |
|----------|----------------|--------------|
| **Time** | Hours on manual tasks | Minutes on automated tasks |
| **Emotion** | Frustration, anxiety, overwhelm | Confidence, calm, satisfaction |
| **Quality** | Errors, inconsistency, rework | Accuracy, consistency, right the first time |
| **Focus** | Scattered across busywork | Focused on high-value work |
| **Relationships** | Tension with team/clients | Smooth collaboration |

**Tips for writing compelling day-in-the-life scenarios:**
- Use a specific persona name and role (not "the user")
- Include sensory details: "She stares at the loading spinner, tapping her pen on the desk"
- Show emotional moments: sighs, smiles, relief
- Include interactions with other people (boss, colleague, client) to add stakes
- End the "with" scenario with a moment of reflection: the character notices how different things are

## 7. User Story Collection Process

You cannot tell great stories without raw material. This section outlines how to systematically collect stories from your users.

### Step 1: Identify Story Candidates

| Source | What to Look For | How to Find Them |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Support tickets** | "This saved me!" moments, before/after descriptions | Tag or flag positive tickets |
| **NPS responses** | Promoters (9-10 scores) with detailed comments | Export NPS data, filter for detail |
| **Social mentions** | Unsolicited praise, use-case descriptions | Social listening tools |
| **Sales calls** | "Why did you choose us?" answers, success follow-ups | Ask sales to flag great stories |
| **Product analytics** | Power users, milestone achievements, rapid adoption | Filter for engagement outliers |
| **Community** | Forum posts, Discord messages, user group discussions | Monitor community channels |
| **Reviews** | Detailed reviews on G2, Capterra, App Store, etc. | Export and analyze review data |

### Step 2: Reach Out

**Email template for requesting a story:**

```
Subject: Can we share your {{PROJECT_NAME}} story?

Hi {{CUSTOMER_NAME}},

We noticed you've been getting great results with {{PROJECT_NAME}} --
{{SPECIFIC_OBSERVATION}}.

We'd love to feature your story to help others facing similar challenges.
It would involve a {{TIME_COMMITMENT}} conversation where we ask about
your experience. We handle all the writing and you approve everything
before it goes live.

As a thank-you, we'd {{INCENTIVE}}.

Would you be open to a quick chat? I'm happy to work around your schedule.

Best,
{{YOUR_NAME}}
```

**What to offer in exchange:**
- Feature their company/logo prominently on your site
- Early access to new features
- Extended trial or account credit
- Co-branded content promotion
- Gift card or swag (check your budget)

### Step 3: Interview Questions

**Warm-up (2 minutes):**
1. Tell me a bit about your role and what your team does.
2. How long have you been using {{PROJECT_NAME}}?

**The problem (5 minutes):**
3. What were you doing before {{PROJECT_NAME}}? Walk me through a typical day.
4. What was the most frustrating part of that process?
5. How did this problem affect your team / your business?
6. Had you tried other solutions? What happened?

**The discovery (3 minutes):**
7. How did you first hear about {{PROJECT_NAME}}?
8. What made you decide to try it?
9. Was there anything that almost stopped you from signing up?

**The experience (5 minutes):**
10. Walk me through your first week with {{PROJECT_NAME}}.
11. When was the moment you knew this was working?
12. What feature or aspect has made the biggest difference?

**The results (5 minutes):**
13. What has changed since you started using {{PROJECT_NAME}}?
14. Can you put a number on the improvement? (time saved, revenue gained, errors reduced)
15. How has this impacted you personally? How do you feel about your work now?

**The recommendation (2 minutes):**
16. Who would you recommend {{PROJECT_NAME}} to?
17. What would you tell someone who is on the fence?
18. If {{PROJECT_NAME}} disappeared tomorrow, what would you miss most?

### Step 4: Turn Interviews into Content

| Interview Insight | Content Type | Where to Use |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------|
| A compelling quote | Pull quote | Landing page, social, ads |
| Before/after data | Metric graphic | Case study, sales deck |
| Emotional moment | Narrative paragraph | Blog post, email, video script |
| Specific result | Stat callout | Homepage, feature page, pricing page |
| Recommendation | Testimonial | Review site, website, email signature |

## 8. Emotional Triggers by Product Type

Different products trigger different emotions. Understand which emotions drive your customers' decisions and lean into them in your storytelling.

### Universal Emotional Triggers

| Emotion | Trigger | Story Element | Example |
|---------|---------|--------------|---------|
| **Relief** | Problem solved after long struggle | The "finally" moment | "After months of manual work, she ran one command and everything just... worked." |
| **Pride** | Accomplishment, recognition | Achievement scene | "When the CEO asked how they shipped so fast, she showed them the dashboard." |

## 9. Story Formats by Channel

The same story needs different shapes for different channels. Here is how to adapt a single customer story across every major format.

### Tweet Thread (X/Twitter)

```
Thread structure (5-7 tweets):

1/ [Hook -- surprising result or bold claim]
   "{{TWEET_THREAD_HOOK}}"

2/ [The problem -- make it relatable]
   "{{TWEET_THREAD_PROBLEM}}"

3/ [What they tried -- show the journey]
   "{{TWEET_THREAD_TRIED}}"

4/ [The turning point -- discovery of {{PROJECT_NAME}}]
   "{{TWEET_THREAD_TURNING_POINT}}"

5/ [The result -- specific numbers]
   "{{TWEET_THREAD_RESULT}}"

6/ [The lesson -- what others can learn]
   "{{TWEET_THREAD_LESSON}}"

7/ [CTA -- where to learn more]
   "{{TWEET_THREAD_CTA}}"
```

**Tweet thread rules:**
- First tweet must stand alone -- it is the hook
- Each tweet should make sense independently (people jump in mid-thread)
- Use numbers and specifics -- vague tweets get scrolled past
- End with a clear CTA or question to drive engagement

## 10. Visual Storytelling Guidance

Words are not the only way to tell stories. Visual elements amplify your narrative and make it memorable.

### Types of Visual Stories

| Visual Type | When to Use | Tips |
|-------------|-------------|------|
| **Screenshots** | Showing the product in action | Annotate with arrows/highlights pointing to the key feature. Crop tightly -- no full-screen shots unless necessary. |
| **Before/after images** | Contrasting the old way vs new way | Use side-by-side layout. Label clearly. Same framing for both. |
| **Diagrams/flowcharts** | Explaining process improvements | Show the "before" process (messy, many steps) vs "after" process (clean, fewer steps). |
| **Data visualizations** | Proving quantified value | Use the simplest chart type possible. Highlight the key number. Cite the source. |
| **Customer photos** | Humanizing testimonials | Real photos beat stock photos every time. Show them in their natural environment. |
| **Product GIFs/videos** | Demonstrating speed and ease | Keep under 15 seconds. Focus on one action. Loop cleanly. |
| **Infographics** | Summarizing complex information | One key message per infographic. Scannable in 10 seconds. Shareable format. |
| **User-generated content** | Building authenticity | Repost with permission. Raw and authentic beats polished. |

### Visual Story Guidelines

**Screenshots:**
- Use the product with realistic data, not placeholder content ("John Smith" not "Test User 123")
- Blur or redact any sensitive information
- Add a subtle shadow or border for polish
- Show the product in context (browser frame for web apps, phone frame for mobile apps)
- Annotate with callouts that describe the BENEFIT, not the feature: "See all your metrics in one place" not "Dashboard view"

**Before/After Visuals:**
- Use identical framing, lighting, and composition for both images
- Label "Before" and "After" clearly
- Consider a slider effect for web (interactive before/after)
- Include a timestamp or context for authenticity

**Data Visualizations:**
- Bar charts for comparisons, line charts for trends
- Highlight the key number with color or size
- Include the exact number as text, not just the visual
- Cite the source: "Based on data from {{DATA_SOURCE}}, {{DATE_RANGE}}"
- Use your brand colors for positive metrics, muted colors for the baseline

**Customer Photos:**
- Real environment beats studio
- Action shots beat posed shots
- Include their name and title for credibility
- Get written permission for commercial use

### Visual Asset Checklist

For each story you create, consider which visuals would strengthen it:

- [ ] Hero image or header graphic
- [ ] Product screenshot showing the key feature
- [ ] Data visualization proving the result
- [ ] Customer photo or company logo
- [ ] Before/after comparison
- [ ] Process diagram (if workflow is part of the story)
- [ ] Pull quote graphic for social sharing
- [ ] Video thumbnail (if video version exists)
