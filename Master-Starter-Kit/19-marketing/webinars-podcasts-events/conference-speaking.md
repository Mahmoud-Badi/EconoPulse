## Why Conference Speaking Matters

### Authority and Credibility
- Being selected to speak at a conference is third-party validation of your expertise.
- "Speaker at {{CONFERENCE_NAME}}" is permanent resume and brand credibility.
- Audiences trust speakers more than marketers — you earn the right to be heard.
- Speaking builds your personal brand, which extends to {{PROJECT_NAME}}.

### Networking
- Conferences concentrate your target audience and industry peers in one place.
- Speakers get access to VIP events, speaker dinners, and green rooms.
- Other speakers often become collaborators, advisors, or partners.
- You meet people who would never respond to a cold email.

### Lead Generation
- A 30-minute talk to 200 people is 100 hours of one-on-one attention equivalent.
- Attendees self-select by being there — they are already interested in your topic.
- Post-talk hallway conversations are high-quality leads.
- Your talk creates a reason for follow-up that feels natural, not salesy.

### Content Creation
- Every talk becomes a blog post, social thread, video, and set of slides.
- Conference organizers often record and publish talks (free distribution).
- Slides can be shared on SlideShare, Speaker Deck, or your website.
- The preparation process forces you to crystalize your thinking.

### PR and Media
- Conference talks can lead to press coverage, especially at industry events.
- Other speakers and attendees reference and share your talk.
- Organizers promote speakers through their channels (free marketing).

## Types of Speaking Opportunities

### Keynotes
- **What:** Main-stage presentation to the entire conference audience.
- **Duration:** 30-60 minutes.
- **Difficulty to land:** Very high — reserved for well-known speakers or groundbreaking topics.
- **Best for:** Broad thought leadership, company announcements, inspiring stories.
- **Tip for newcomers:** Build toward keynotes by speaking at smaller events first.

### Breakout Sessions
- **What:** Focused talk in a specific track or room.
- **Duration:** 25-45 minutes.
- **Difficulty to land:** Moderate — most conferences have many breakout slots.
- **Best for:** Technical deep-dives, case studies, specific how-tos.
- **This is your starting point** for most conference speaking careers.

### Panels
- **What:** Moderated discussion with 3-5 speakers on a shared topic.
- **Duration:** 30-60 minutes.
- **Difficulty to land:** Moderate to low — organizers need to fill multiple seats.
- **Best for:** Sharing perspectives, industry discussions, debates.
- **Tips:** Be concise (limited talking time), make memorable points, don't dominate.

### Workshops
- **What:** Hands-on session where attendees practice or build something.
- **Duration:** 90 minutes to full day.
- **Difficulty to land:** Moderate — requires strong teaching ability.
- **Best for:** Teaching skills related to {{PROJECT_NAME}}, live coding, hands-on exercises.
- **Bonus:** Workshop attendees have the highest engagement and conversion rate.

### Lightning Talks
- **What:** Short, focused presentations.
- **Duration:** 5-10 minutes.
- **Difficulty to land:** Low — many conferences have open lightning talk slots.
- **Best for:** Getting started, testing ideas, practicing, gaining visibility.
- **Strategy:** Use lightning talks to get comfortable before applying for full sessions.

### Unconferences and Open Spaces
- **What:** Attendee-driven sessions proposed and voted on day-of.
- **Duration:** 30-60 minutes.
- **Difficulty to land:** None — just show up and propose a session.
- **Best for:** Discussions, feedback, community engagement.
- **Great for:** Testing topics and getting immediate audience feedback.

## Writing a Winning CFP

### CFP Anatomy

A CFP (Call for Proposals) typically asks for:
1. Talk title
2. Abstract / description
3. Speaker bio
4. Talk format and duration preference
5. Target audience / skill level
6. Why this talk and why you

### Title: Specific and Intriguing

Your title must communicate value and spark curiosity.

| Bad Title | Why It's Bad | Better Title |
|---|---|---|
| "Introduction to {{TOPIC}}" | Generic, boring | "The {{NUMBER}} {{TOPIC}} Mistakes Costing You {{CONSEQUENCE}}" |
| "{{PROJECT_NAME}} Overview" | Sounds like a sales pitch | "How We {{ACHIEVED_RESULT}} by {{APPROACH}} (and What We Learned)" |
| "Thoughts on {{INDUSTRY}}" | Vague, no clear takeaway | "Why {{CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM}} Is Wrong (And What to Do Instead)" |
| "Best Practices for {{TOPIC}}" | Overused, unoriginal | "From {{BEFORE_STATE}} to {{AFTER_STATE}}: A Practitioner's Guide" |

**Title formulas that work:**
- "How to {{ACHIEVE_RESULT}} Without {{COMMON_APPROACH}}"
- "{{NUMBER}} Lessons from {{EXPERIENCE}} at {{SCALE}}"
- "The {{ADJECTIVE}} Guide to {{TOPIC}} That Nobody Talks About"
- "{{CONTROVERSIAL_TAKE}}: Why We {{ACTION}} and Never Looked Back"

### Abstract: Problem, Angle, Takeaways

Structure your abstract to answer three questions: Why should I attend? What's unique about this? What will I leave with?

```
ABSTRACT TEMPLATE:

[Opening: State the problem or challenge the audience faces]
{{TARGET_AUDIENCE}} struggle with {{PROBLEM}}. Most advice says to
{{COMMON_APPROACH}}, but after {{YOUR_EXPERIENCE}}, we discovered
that {{INSIGHT}}.

[Middle: Your unique angle or approach]
In this talk, I'll share {{WHAT_YOU'LL_SHARE}} based on
{{CREDIBILITY_SOURCE}} (building {{PROJECT_NAME}}, analyzing
{{DATA}}, working with {{NUMBER}} customers).

[Closing: What attendees will learn / takeaways]
Attendees will walk away with:
- {{TAKEAWAY_1}}: A {{FRAMEWORK/TOOL/APPROACH}} they can apply immediately
- {{TAKEAWAY_2}}: Understanding of {{CONCEPT}} and why it matters
- {{TAKEAWAY_3}}: {{SPECIFIC_RESOURCE}} they can reference after the talk

This talk is designed for {{SKILL_LEVEL}} {{TARGET_AUDIENCE}} who
{{SITUATION}}.
```

### Speaker Bio for CFPs

Your bio should establish credibility for this specific topic, not just general background.

```
BIO TEMPLATE:

{{YOUR_NAME}} is the {{ROLE}} of {{PROJECT_NAME}}, a
{{PRODUCT_DESCRIPTION}} that has {{TRACTION_POINT}}. With
{{YEARS}} years of experience in {{RELEVANT_AREA}},
{{YOUR_NAME}} has {{RELEVANT_ACHIEVEMENT_1}} and
{{RELEVANT_ACHIEVEMENT_2}}.

{{YOUR_NAME}} has previously spoken at {{PAST_EVENT_1}} and
{{PAST_EVENT_2}}, and is a regular contributor to
{{PUBLICATION/COMMUNITY}}.

[If no past speaking experience:]
{{YOUR_NAME}} brings a practitioner's perspective to
{{TOPIC}}, sharing real lessons from building and scaling
{{PROJECT_NAME}} in {{INDUSTRY}}.
```

### Common CFP Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Gets Rejected | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Too salesy | Reviewers protect their audience from ads | Focus on teaching, not promotion |
| Too vague | "I'll talk about trends" — which trends? Why? | Be specific about content and takeaways |
| Not audience-focused | Written from your perspective, not attendees' | Frame everything as audience value |
| Too basic | Covers what everyone already knows | Offer unique insights, data, or perspective |
| Too advanced without context | Assumes knowledge most attendees lack | Specify target audience and prerequisites |
| No clear takeaways | Attendees can't articulate what they'll learn | List 3-5 concrete things they'll walk away with |
| Generic bio | Doesn't establish credibility for this topic | Tailor bio to the specific talk topic |

## Talk Preparation

### Story Arc Structure

The best conference talks follow a narrative arc, not a list of bullet points.

```
1. THE HOOK (2-3 min)
   - Open with a surprising statistic, story, or question
   - Establish why this topic matters RIGHT NOW
   - Preview what the audience will learn

2. THE PROBLEM (5-7 min)
   - Paint the current pain point clearly
   - Share a specific example (your own experience or customer story)
   - Make the audience feel the problem ("Have you ever...?")

3. THE JOURNEY (10-15 min)
   - Walk through the process of discovery
   - Share what you tried, what failed, and why
   - Include honest moments — vulnerability builds trust

4. THE INSIGHT (5-10 min)
   - The key breakthrough or framework
   - This is the core value of your talk
   - Make it concrete and actionable

5. THE RESOLUTION (5-7 min)
   - Show the result of applying the insight
   - Data, metrics, before/after comparisons
   - Customer story or case study

6. THE ACTION ITEMS (3-5 min)
   - 3-5 specific things the audience can do tomorrow
   - Make them concrete, not abstract
   - Point to resources for going deeper

7. THE CLOSE (1-2 min)
   - Circle back to your opening hook
   - End with a memorable line or call-to-action
   - Thank the audience
```

### Slide Design Principles

- **One idea per slide.** If you need two points, use two slides.
- **Minimal text.** Maximum 6 words per line, 6 lines per slide (6x6 rule). Fewer is better.
- **High-impact visuals.** Photos, diagrams, charts — not clip art or stock photos.
- **Consistent brand.** Use {{PROJECT_NAME}} brand colors and fonts throughout.
- **Readable from the back row.** Minimum 30pt font for body text, 48pt for titles.
- **Dark background, light text** for projector readability in most conference venues.
- **No walls of code.** If showing code, highlight only the relevant 3-5 lines.
- **Slide numbers.** Help people reference specific slides in Q&A.
- **Speaker notes.** For your reference during practice, not to read from.

### Practice Protocol

1. **Dry run 1 (solo):** Read through with slides. Identify flow issues and timing.
2. **Dry run 2 (recorded):** Record yourself. Watch it back — painful but essential.
3. **Dry run 3 (small audience):** Present to 2-3 trusted people. Get honest feedback.
4. **Final run (dress rehearsal):** Full run-through with final slides, standing, with timer.

**Timing rule:** Plan for 80% of your allotted time. If you have 30 minutes, plan for 24. You will always run slightly longer than practice, and you need buffer for Q&A.

## At the Conference

### Networking Strategy

**Before the event:**
- Review the speaker list and attendee list (if available).
- Identify 5-10 specific people you want to meet.
- Connect on Twitter/LinkedIn before the event: "Looking forward to {{CONFERENCE_NAME}}! Will I see you there?"
- Plan your schedule around the people you want to meet, not just the talks.

**At the event:**
- Arrive early to sessions — hallway conversations before talks start are gold.
- Sit at communal tables during meals and coffee breaks.
- Conversation starters: "What brought you to this conference?" "What's been the best session so far?" "What are you working on?"
- Exchange contact info (LinkedIn connect on the spot, or swap cards if you have them).
- Take notes on who you meet and what you discussed (for follow-up).

**At social events:**
- Speaker dinners, after-parties, and hallway tracks are where real connections happen.
- Be genuinely curious about other people — don't just talk about yourself.
- Offer to help or connect people before asking for anything.

### Talk Day Logistics

- [ ] Arrive at your room 15-20 minutes early.
- [ ] Test laptop connection with projector/screen.
- [ ] Check microphone (lapel mic? handheld? podium?).
- [ ] Have slides loaded on your computer AND on a USB drive (backup).
- [ ] Close all notifications, open only presentation software.
- [ ] Know where the timer or time signals are.
- [ ] Have water at the podium.
- [ ] Check slide advancer (bring your own if possible — Logitech Spotlight is excellent).

### Talk Promotion

- Ask a friend or colleague in the audience to live-tweet key points.
- Share your slides URL at the start of the talk so attendees can follow along.
- Tweet before your talk: "About to take the stage at {{CONFERENCE_NAME}} to talk about {{TOPIC}}! Come to Room {{ROOM}} at {{TIME}}."
- After the talk, post a photo from stage with a key takeaway.

### Lead Capture During the Talk

- **CTA slide:** Include a clear next step. Not "visit our website" but "Go to {{PROJECT_NAME}}.com/{{CONFERENCE_SLUG}} for the template I shared today."
- **QR code:** Display a QR code linking to your landing page, lead magnet, or special offer.
- **Offer something exclusive:** "Conference attendees get {{OFFER}} — just use code {{CONFERENCE_CODE}}."
- **Slides link:** "Get these slides at {{SLIDES_URL}}" — captures interest and creates a touchpoint.

```

Really enjoyed our conversation about {{SPECIFIC_TOPIC}} at
{{CONFERENCE_NAME}} {{CONTEXT_DETAIL — "at the speaker dinner"
/ "after the keynote" / "in the hallway"}}.

{{PERSONAL_FOLLOW_UP — reference something specific they said
or offer something you promised}}.

Would love to stay connected — {{NEXT_STEP: "grab coffee next
time I'm in {{CITY}}" / "continue our chat about {{TOPIC}}" /
"share the resource I mentioned"}}.

Best,
{{YOUR_NAME}}
{{PROJECT_NAME}} — {{WEBSITE_URL}}
```

**For the conference organizer:**
```
Subject: Thanks for {{CONFERENCE_NAME}} — great experience

Hi {{ORGANIZER_NAME}},

Just wanted to say thanks for having me speak at
{{CONFERENCE_NAME}}. It was a fantastic experience and the
audience was incredibly engaged.

I'd love to be considered for future events. Happy to present
on a new topic or do a workshop next time.

Thanks again for all the work you put into making it great!

{{YOUR_NAME}}
```

### Share Your Talk

- Upload slides to Speaker Deck, SlideShare, or your website.
- If the conference recorded the talk, share the video widely once it's published.
- Write a blog post expanding on the key points from your talk.
- Create a Twitter thread summarizing your talk's main insights.
- Add the talk to your website's speaking page.

### Content Repurposing

```
CONFERENCE TALK
├── Slides → Speaker Deck / SlideShare / PDF download
├── Recording → YouTube (if you have it)
├── Blog post → detailed write-up of key points
├── Twitter thread → key takeaways in thread format
├── LinkedIn post → reflections and lessons from the talk
├── Email to list → "I spoke at {{CONFERENCE}} — here's what I shared"
├── Short clips → social media highlights (if video available)
└── Updated talk → iterate for the next conference
```
