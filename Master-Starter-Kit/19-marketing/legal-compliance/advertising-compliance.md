# Advertising Compliance

> FTC disclosure requirements, testimonial rules, affiliate guidelines, and advertising regulations for digital marketing.

---

## FTC Endorsement Guidelines

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) requires that advertising be truthful, not deceptive, and that endorsements reflect honest opinions. These rules apply to all marketing — social media, website, email, ads, and influencer content.

### Core Principles
1. Endorsements must reflect honest opinions of the endorser
2. Material connections between endorsers and brands must be disclosed
3. Claims must be substantiated — if an endorser makes a claim, the advertiser must have proof
4. Disclosures must be clear, conspicuous, and unavoidable

## Disclosure Requirements

### When Disclosure Is Required
- Paid partnerships (money, free product, affiliate commission)
- Employee posts about their own company
- Influencer posts where any compensation was exchanged
- Affiliate links where you earn commission
- Sponsored content of any kind
- Reviews where the product was provided free

### How to Disclose

**Social Media:**
- Use clear language: #ad, #sponsored, #partner (not #sp, #collab, #ambassador alone)
- Place disclosure at the beginning of the post, not buried at the end
- On video: verbal disclosure at the start AND written overlay
- Instagram/TikTok: use the platform's built-in "Paid Partnership" label PLUS your own disclosure

**Blog Posts:**
- Clear statement at the top: "This post contains affiliate links. I may earn a commission at no extra cost to you."
- For sponsored posts: "This post is sponsored by [Brand]. All opinions are my own."
- For gifted products: "I received this product free for review. My opinions are honest and unbiased."

**Email:**
- Sponsored content clearly labeled: "[Sponsored]" or "In partnership with [Brand]"
- Affiliate links disclosed: "This email contains affiliate links."

**Website:**
- Affiliate disclosure page linked from footer
- Individual page disclosures near affiliate content
- Comparison pages: disclose if you earn affiliate commission from recommended products

### Disclosure Examples
```
Good:  "#ad I've been using [Product] for 3 months and love it. [Brand] is paying me to share this."
Bad:   "Love this product! #blessed #partner" (buried, vague)

Good:  "Disclosure: This post contains affiliate links. I earn a small commission if you purchase."
Bad:   "Some links may be affiliate links." (too vague)

Good:  "Sponsored by [Brand]. I received payment for this review. All opinions are my own."
Bad:   "Thanks to [Brand] for making this possible." (doesn't clearly state it's paid)
```

## Testimonial Rules

### Requirements
- Testimonials must be from real people who actually used the product
- Results described must be typical OR clearly disclosed as atypical
- Fabricated testimonials are illegal — including AI-generated fake reviews
- You cannot pay for positive reviews without disclosure

### "Typical Results" Disclaimer
If a testimonial shows exceptional results:
```
"I grew my business 300% using [Product]"
* Results not typical. Individual results vary based on effort, market conditions, and other factors.
  Typical users see [X-Y]% improvement.
```

### What You Can and Cannot Do
| Action | Allowed? |
|--------|----------|
| Use real customer quotes with permission | Yes |
| Edit for grammar without changing meaning | Yes |
| Cherry-pick only positive testimonials | Yes, but atypical results need disclaimers |
| Create composite testimonials from multiple customers | No |
| Use fake names with real quotes | Generally no — use real names or "verified user" |
| Use AI-generated fake testimonials | No — illegal |
| Incentivize reviews (discount for review) | Yes, but must disclose the incentive |

## Comparative Advertising

### Rules for Comparison Claims
- Comparisons must be truthful and substantiated
- Compare on the same basis (don't compare your paid plan to competitor's free plan)
- Don't disparage competitors with false claims
- Name competitors only if claims are verifiable
- "Better than X" requires objective evidence

### Safe Comparison Formats
- Feature comparison tables with verifiable, current data
- "Alternative to [Competitor]" positioning (factual, not disparaging)
- Price comparison with clear tier matching
- Performance benchmarks with methodology disclosed

### Risky Comparison Formats
- "We're better than X" without specific evidence
- Using outdated competitor information
- Cherry-picking the one metric where you win
- Implying competitor is unsafe, unreliable, or going out of business

## Health, Environmental, and Performance Claims

### Performance Claims
- "Saves 10 hours per week" — must be substantiated with data or user research
- "99.9% uptime" — must reflect actual measured uptime
- "Fastest" — must be verifiable through benchmarks

### Environmental Claims (Green Marketing)
- "Eco-friendly," "sustainable," "green" — must be specific and substantiated
- "Carbon neutral" — requires third-party certification
- Vague environmental claims without evidence are illegal in many jurisdictions

### Health and Safety Claims
- Cannot make health claims for non-medical products
- "Reduces stress" or "improves mental health" require substantiation
- FDA and FTC jointly enforce health claim regulations

## "Free" Offer Rules

- "Free trial" must actually be free — no hidden charges during the trial
- If credit card is required, disclose clearly what happens after the free period
- "Free" with mandatory additional purchase must be disclosed ("Free with purchase of X")
- "Free forever" plans must actually remain free (or clearly state conditions for change)
- FTC considers it deceptive to advertise "free" if the product's cost is built into something else

---

## Native Advertising / Sponsored Content

- Must be clearly labeled as advertising or sponsored
- The label must be visible without scrolling or clicking
- Use: "Sponsored," "Ad," "Paid Content," "Promoted"
- Don't use: "Presented by," "Brought to you by" (insufficient in many cases)
- Content should not mimic editorial content without clear identification

---

## Social Media Advertising Rules by Platform

### Facebook/Instagram (Meta)
- Must use "Paid Partnership" tag for branded content
- Ad copy must comply with Meta's advertising policies
- No misleading before/after images
- No ads targeting based on sensitive categories (health, race, religion)

### Twitter/X
- Promoted content labeled automatically
- Organic sponsored posts still need #ad disclosure
- No deceptive engagement tactics

### LinkedIn
- Sponsored content labeled automatically
- Employee advocacy posts about their company should disclose employment
- Thought leadership posts promoting own product should be transparent

### TikTok
- Must use "Paid Partnership" label
- Must comply with TikTok's branded content policy
- Disclosure must be in the video, not just the caption

## Influencer Marketing Compliance

### Your Responsibilities as a Brand
1. Require disclosure in all influencer agreements
2. Monitor influencer posts for compliance
3. Have a written influencer agreement that includes disclosure requirements
4. Don't suggest influencers hide the commercial relationship
5. You're liable if your influencers don't disclose — not just them

### Influencer Agreement Should Include
- Clear disclosure requirements with examples
- Approval process for content before posting
- Truthfulness requirements (don't require positive-only reviews)
- FTC compliance acknowledgment
- Content ownership and usage rights

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

| Violation | Potential Penalty |
|-----------|------------------|
| Deceptive advertising (FTC) | Up to $50,120 per violation |
| Fake reviews | FTC enforcement action + fines |
| Missing affiliate disclosure | FTC warning letter → fines |
| False health claims | FDA/FTC joint enforcement |
| Misleading pricing | State AG enforcement |

## Compliance Checklist

- [ ] All paid partnerships and sponsorships clearly disclosed
- [ ] Affiliate links have visible disclosure near them
- [ ] Testimonials are from real customers with documented permission
- [ ] Atypical results have "results not typical" disclaimers
- [ ] Comparison claims are factual, current, and verifiable
- [ ] "Free" offers have no hidden charges or conditions
- [ ] Performance claims are substantiated with data
- [ ] Influencer agreements include FTC disclosure requirements
- [ ] Native advertising is clearly labeled
- [ ] Social media ads comply with platform-specific policies
- [ ] Pricing displays are transparent and not misleading
- [ ] Environmental claims are specific and substantiated
