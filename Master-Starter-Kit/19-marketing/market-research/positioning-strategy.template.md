## 1. Positioning Fundamentals

### What Positioning IS

Positioning is the act of designing your company's offering and image to occupy a distinctive place in the mind of your target market. It is not what you do to a product -- it is what you do to the mind of the prospect.

### What Positioning is NOT

- It is NOT a tagline (though a tagline should reflect your positioning)
- It is NOT a list of features (though features support positioning)
- It is NOT aspirational branding (it must be grounded in truth)
- It is NOT permanent (positioning evolves as the market and your product change)

### The Three Requirements of Strong Positioning

1. **Relevant:** It addresses something your target audience genuinely cares about.
2. **Differentiated:** It sets you apart from alternatives in a meaningful way.
3. **Credible:** You can actually deliver on the claim today, not just in the future.

## 2. Positioning Statement

### The Positioning Statement Formula

```
For [target customer]
who [statement of need or opportunity],
{{PROJECT_NAME}} is the [product category]
that [key benefit / reason to buy]
unlike [primary competitive alternative]
because [reason to believe / key differentiator].
```

### Building Your Positioning Statement

#### Step 1: Define Each Component

| Component | Your Input | Guidance |
|-----------|-----------|----------|

## 3. Category Strategy

One of the most important positioning decisions is which category to compete in.

### Option A: Compete in an Existing Category

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Customers already understand the category | You are compared directly to established players |
| Existing demand and search volume | Must differentiate on execution or niche |
| Easier to explain what you do | Harder to stand out in a crowded market |
| Analyst coverage and review sites exist | Existing leaders have advantages |

**When to choose:** You have a clear, defensible differentiator within an established category (better for X, cheaper for Y, faster at Z).

### Option B: Create a Sub-Category

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Reduces direct competition | Smaller perceived market |
| Positions you as the leader of a niche | Requires educating the market on the sub-category |
| Focused messaging resonates more | May limit growth if niche is too small |
| Easier to dominate search for the sub-category | Risk that the sub-category is not real or sustainable |

**When to choose:** The existing category is too broad, and your product serves a specific audience or use case significantly better than general solutions.

**Examples of sub-category creation:**
- "Project management" → "Project management for creative agencies"
- "CRM" → "CRM for solo consultants"
- "Email marketing" → "Email marketing for e-commerce"

### Option C: Create an Entirely New Category

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| No direct competitors | Massive education burden |
| You define the rules | No existing demand or search volume |
| Potential for outsized returns if category takes off | High risk -- category may never take hold |
| PR and analyst interest in new categories | Requires significant marketing investment |

**When to choose:** Your product is genuinely different enough that forcing it into an existing category misrepresents what it does, and you have the resources to educate the market.

**Examples of successful new categories:**
- Drift created "Conversational Marketing"
- Gong created "Revenue Intelligence"
- Figma expanded "Design" into "Collaborative Design"

## 7. Message-Market Fit Testing

Your positioning only works if it resonates with your audience. Test it before committing.

### Testing Framework

| Test Method | What It Tests | Sample Size | Timeline | Cost |
|-------------|--------------|-------------|----------|------|
| 5-second test | Clarity of value proposition | 20-50 people | 1-2 days | Free-$100 |
| Landing page A/B test | Which positioning drives action | 500+ visitors per variant | 2-4 weeks | Ad spend |
| Social media poll | Audience preference between options | 100+ votes | 1-3 days | Free |
| Customer interview | Deep resonance and objections | 5-10 people | 1-2 weeks | Free |
| Ad copy testing | Which message gets clicks | 1000+ impressions per variant | 1-2 weeks | $200-500 |
| Email subject line test | Which framing gets opens | 1000+ recipients | 1 send | Free |
| Positioning statement test | Comprehension and appeal | 20-50 people | 1-2 days | Free |

### 5-Second Test Script

Show your homepage or positioning statement for 5 seconds, then ask:

1. "What does this product do?"
2. "Who is it for?"
3. "How is it different from alternatives?"
4. "Would you want to learn more? Why or why not?"

**Target: 80%+ of respondents should be able to answer questions 1 and 2 correctly.**

### SaaS Positioning Strategies

**Common SaaS Positioning Archetypes:**

| Archetype | Description | Example | Best For |

| The Integrator | "The [category] that connects all your tools" | Zapier, Make | Fragmented tool stacks |
| The AI-Native | "AI-powered [category]" | Jasper, Copy.ai | Categories ripe for AI |
| The Platform | "Build your own [workflow] on our platform" | Airtable, Retool | Power users who need flexibility |

**SaaS-Specific Positioning Tips:**
- Lead with outcomes, not features. "Close deals faster" beats "AI-powered CRM."
- Free trials reduce risk -- position them as "see for yourself" confidence.
- Integration ecosystem is a competitive moat -- highlight it.
- Customer logos and case studies are the most powerful proof in B2B SaaS.
- Position your pricing as "aligned with your success" (usage-based, per-seat, etc.).
