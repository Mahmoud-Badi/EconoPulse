# Growth Mistakes

> Mistakes that stall growth after launch — premature scaling, wrong channels, and vanity metrics.

---

## Mistake 1: Premature Scaling

**The mistake:** Pouring money into paid ads, hiring a marketing team, or scaling distribution before you have product-market fit.

**The consequence:** You amplify a broken funnel. More traffic to a product with 5% activation rate just means more wasted money. You burn through budget without learning anything.

**The fix:**
- Don't scale until activation rate > 25% and 30-day retention > 20%
- Fix the funnel before filling it — optimize onboarding, reduce churn, improve activation
- Growth = product-market fit first, then distribution. Never the reverse.
- Ask yourself: "If I 10x traffic tomorrow, would the product handle it well?" If not, you're not ready.

---

## Mistake 2: Wrong Channel Selection

**The mistake:** Investing heavily in a channel that doesn't match your audience. Running TikTok ads for enterprise B2B software. Writing blog posts for a consumer app with a teenage audience.

**The consequence:** Wasted time and budget. Low-quality leads that don't convert. Frustration and the false conclusion that "marketing doesn't work for us."

**The fix:**
- Go where your customers already are, not where you're comfortable
- Test 2-3 channels with small experiments ($200 budget, 2 weeks each) before committing
- Use the channel decision tree (see `channel-decision-tree.md`) matched to your product type
- Talk to your best customers: "How did you find us? What do you read/watch/follow?"

---

## Mistake 3: Vanity Metrics Obsession

**The mistake:** Celebrating Twitter followers, page views, and newsletter subscribers while ignoring activation, retention, and revenue.

**The consequence:** You feel like you're growing but revenue is flat. The metrics that look good on a dashboard don't translate to business outcomes.

**The fix:**
- Track the metrics that matter: activated users, MRR, churn rate, LTV, CAC
- For every vanity metric, define the conversion to a real metric ("1,000 blog visitors → X signups → Y activated → Z paying")
- If a channel generates traffic but not signups, the problem is either the channel or the landing page — diagnose which
- Weekly review should focus on revenue metrics first, awareness metrics second

---

## Mistake 4: Not Doubling Down on What Works

**The mistake:** You find a channel that's working (SEO is driving 50 signups/month) but instead of scaling it, you chase new channels.

**The consequence:** You never fully exploit your best channel. The new channels underperform because they're unproven. Your best channel stagnates from neglect.

**The fix:**
- When something works, double down before diversifying
- Can you 3x output on your best channel? (More content, more budget, more optimization)
- Only add a new channel after your best channel is fully leveraged or plateauing
- The 80/20 rule applies: 80% effort on proven channels, 20% on experiments

---

## Mistake 5: Spreading Too Thin Across Channels

**The mistake:** Posting on Twitter, LinkedIn, YouTube, TikTok, Reddit, Indie Hackers, your blog, a newsletter, and a podcast — all at mediocre quality.

**The consequence:** None of your channels build momentum. Each one gets 10% of your effort and produces 10% of potential results. You burn out without seeing results.

**The fix:**
- Pick 2-3 channels maximum and do them exceptionally well
- One primary channel (where most of your audience is) and one secondary
- Master one channel before adding another
- It's better to post 5x/week on Twitter than 1x/week on 5 platforms

---

## Mistake 6: Ignoring Retention for Acquisition

**The mistake:** Obsessing over new user signups while existing users are churning at 10%/month.

**The consequence:** You're filling a leaky bucket. Net growth is zero or negative. You spend more to acquire users than you keep. LTV drops, making every channel unprofitable.

**The fix:**
- Fix retention before scaling acquisition — always
- A 5% improvement in retention has a larger revenue impact than a 20% increase in signups
- Talk to churned users: "Why did you leave?" The answers are worth more than any acquisition strategy
- Set a retention target before spending on growth (e.g., 30-day retention > 40%)

---

## Mistake 7: No Referral Program

**The mistake:** Your happiest users have no easy way to refer others. No referral link, no incentive, no prompt.

**The consequence:** You miss free, high-quality growth. Referred users have higher LTV, lower CAC, and better retention than any other channel.

**The fix:**
- Add a simple referral mechanism (unique link, invite email, share button)
- Incentivize both sides: referrer gets X, new user gets Y
- Prompt referrals at moments of delight (after a success, after a milestone, after a positive NPS score)
- Even a basic "Share with a friend" button with no incentive captures some referral traffic

---

## Mistake 8: Not Automating What's Working

**The mistake:** You manually do something that works (write personalized onboarding emails, manually post social content, individually message new signups) but never systematize it.

**The consequence:** The activity stops when you get busy. It doesn't scale. You become the bottleneck.

**The fix:**
- If you do something manually more than 10 times and it works, automate it
- Email sequences replace manual onboarding emails
- Buffer/Hootsuite replace manual social posting
- Zapier/Make replace manual data entry between tools
- Automation frees you to work on the next growth lever

---

## Mistake 9: Copying Competitor Strategies Blindly

**The mistake:** "Competitor X runs Google Ads, so we should too." "They have a podcast, we need a podcast."

**The consequence:** You copy their channel without understanding their context (budget, team size, audience maturity). What works for a funded company with 5 marketers won't work for a solo founder.

**The fix:**
- Analyze WHY competitors use certain channels, not just WHICH channels
- Consider your unique advantages — what can you do that they can't?
- A solo founder's advantage is authenticity, speed, and personal touch — leverage those
- Competitors' strategies may not even be working — you only see the visible activity, not the results

---

## Mistake 10: Ignoring Unit Economics

**The mistake:** Growing revenue without tracking whether each customer is profitable. Your CAC is $50 but your LTV is $40.

**The consequence:** You lose money on every customer and try to make it up with volume. Growth actually accelerates losses.

**The fix:**
- Know your numbers: CAC (customer acquisition cost), LTV (lifetime value), payback period
- LTV must be 3x+ CAC for sustainable growth
- If LTV < CAC, either reduce acquisition costs, increase pricing, or improve retention — don't scale
- Track CAC by channel — some channels may be profitable while others aren't

---

## Mistake 11: Not Building Community

**The mistake:** Treating users as transactions instead of building a community around your product.

**The consequence:** No word of mouth, no user-generated content, no loyalty beyond the product features. Users leave for the next shiny alternative.

**The fix:**
- Create a space for users to connect (Discord, Slack, forum, or even a shared hashtag)
- Feature user stories and wins publicly
- Give power users early access, input on roadmap, and recognition
- Community members churn less and refer more — it's the best retention tool

---

## Mistake 12: Skipping Email List Building

**The mistake:** Focusing entirely on social media and search traffic without building an email list.

**The consequence:** You don't own your audience. Algorithm changes (Twitter, Google, Instagram) can wipe out your traffic overnight. You have no direct line to your users.

**The fix:**
- Email list is your #1 marketing asset — prioritize it from day one
- Every blog post, landing page, and social profile should drive email signups
- Even 500 engaged subscribers are more valuable than 10,000 social followers
- Email consistently outperforms every other channel for conversion rates

## Growth Stage Checklist

### Before Scaling (Product-Market Fit Phase)
- [ ] Activation rate > 25%
- [ ] 30-day retention > 20%
- [ ] Users can describe the value in their own words
- [ ] At least 10 users would be "very disappointed" if the product disappeared

### Ready to Scale (Growth Phase)
- [ ] LTV > 3x CAC
- [ ] One channel producing consistent, profitable growth
- [ ] Onboarding is automated and optimized
- [ ] Retention is stable or improving month-over-month
- [ ] Referral mechanism is in place

### Scaling (Expansion Phase)
- [ ] Primary channel is maximized, ready to add secondary channels
- [ ] Marketing automation handles repetitive tasks
- [ ] Community or user advocacy program active
- [ ] Email list growing at 10%+ month-over-month
- [ ] Unit economics are clear and sustainable
