# Pricing Mistakes

> Common pricing and monetization errors that kill revenue — and how to fix each one.

---

## Mistake 1: Pricing Too Low

**The mistake:** Setting prices at $5-10/month because you're afraid nobody will pay more.

**The consequence:** You need 10x more customers to reach the same revenue. Low prices attract price-sensitive customers who churn faster and demand more support. You can't afford to invest in growth.

**The fix:**
- Price based on value delivered, not gut feeling or competitor matching
- Ask potential customers: "What would you expect to pay for a tool that [outcome]?"
- Start at 2-3x what feels comfortable — you can always offer discounts
- If fewer than 20% of prospects push back on price, you're too cheap

---

## Mistake 2: Racing to the Bottom

**The mistake:** Competing on price by undercutting competitors. "We're the cheapest option!"

**The consequence:** Price wars destroy margins. You attract the most price-sensitive customers. Anyone can undercut you further. You have no budget for marketing or improvement.

**The fix:**
- Compete on value, not price — find a differentiation axis that isn't cost
- Position as premium with clear value justification
- If your only advantage is "cheaper," you don't have a real product advantage
- Price can be a feature, but it shouldn't be THE feature

---

## Mistake 3: No Free Tier When You Need One

**The mistake:** Requiring payment upfront for a product nobody knows about, in a market where free alternatives exist.

**The consequence:** Zero adoption. Prospects choose competitors who let them try before buying. Your funnel has no top.

**The fix:**
- If your market has free alternatives, you need a free tier or trial
- Free tier works for: self-serve SaaS, developer tools, productivity apps
- Free tier doesn't work for: high-touch B2B, consulting, custom solutions
- Design the free tier as a marketing channel, not a charity — it should create desire for paid features

---

## Mistake 4: Too Generous Free Tier

**The mistake:** Giving away so much in the free tier that nobody needs to upgrade.

**The consequence:** High adoption, zero revenue. Users get 90% of value for free. The upgrade from free to paid doesn't feel worth the price.

**The fix:**
- Free tier should deliver the "aha moment" but create a clear wall before full value
- Limit by usage (100 API calls/month), not by features that feel essential
- Study what actions correlate with conversion — gate things just beyond that point
- The free tier should make users want more, not feel satisfied

---

## Mistake 5: Too Many Pricing Tiers

**The mistake:** Offering 5-7 pricing plans with subtle differences between them.

**The consequence:** Decision paralysis. Prospects can't figure out which plan they need. They leave instead of choosing. Support gets flooded with "which plan do I need?" questions.

**The fix:**
- 3 tiers is the sweet spot: Starter, Pro, Enterprise (or equivalent)
- Each tier should serve a clearly different customer segment
- Make the middle tier the obvious choice (anchor with the expensive tier)
- If you can't clearly explain who each tier is for in one sentence, you have too many

---

## Mistake 6: Hidden Fees That Erode Trust

**The mistake:** Advertising a low base price, then adding mandatory fees at checkout (setup fees, platform fees, transaction fees, overage charges).

**The consequence:** Trust destruction. Customers feel tricked. They leave negative reviews. Higher churn because of billing surprises.

**The fix:**
- Be transparent about total cost on the pricing page
- If you have usage-based pricing, show clear examples of what typical users pay
- Mandatory fees should be included in the advertised price
- Surprise charges are the fastest way to generate negative word of mouth

---

## Mistake 7: Not Testing Pricing

**The mistake:** Setting a price once and never revisiting it, even as the product and market evolve.

**The consequence:** You're likely leaving 30-50% of potential revenue on the table. Market conditions change, your product has more value than when you launched, and competitors have shifted.

**The fix:**
- Review pricing every 6 months
- A/B test pricing pages (not the price itself, but how you present value)
- Survey new customers: "Was the price a factor in your decision? Would you have paid more?"
- Test pricing on new customer segments before changing existing prices

---

## Mistake 8: Annual Discount Too Steep

**The mistake:** Offering 50-60% off for annual billing ("$10/month or $49/year!").

**The consequence:** Massive revenue loss. Annual plans are great for retention, but giving away 6 months of revenue destroys your economics. Most customers who would pay monthly will take the annual deal.

**The fix:**
- Standard annual discount: 15-20% (e.g., $10/month or $96/year = 2 months free)
- Frame it as "2 months free" not "20% off" — the psychology is different
- For early-stage products, monthly billing provides better cash flow signals
- Only go steeper on annual discounts if you have a churn problem you can't solve otherwise

---

## Mistake 9: No Pricing Page

**The mistake:** Forcing prospects to "contact sales" or "request a quote" for a self-serve product.

**The consequence:** You lose 80%+ of potential customers who don't want to talk to a human. "Contact for pricing" signals expensive enterprise software, not indie SaaS.

**The fix:**
- If your product is self-serve, show prices publicly
- "Contact for pricing" is only appropriate for true enterprise deals (>$10K/year) or custom solutions
- Even enterprise products benefit from a "Starting at $X" indicator
- Transparency builds trust and filters out prospects who can't afford you

---

## Mistake 10: Complicated Pricing Models

**The mistake:** Usage-based pricing with 8 different dimensions: "$0.01 per API call + $0.005 per MB stored + $0.10 per compute minute + $0.02 per active user..."

**The consequence:** Prospects can't predict their bill. Unpredictability creates anxiety. They choose a competitor with simpler pricing even if it costs more.

**The fix:**
- Price on one primary dimension that correlates with value received
- If usage-based, provide a calculator and real examples ("Most teams of 10 pay $X/month")
- Offer a flat-rate option for customers who want predictability
- Simplicity is a feature — "unlimited for $49/month" converts better than complex per-unit pricing for most segments

---

## Mistake 11: Not Segmenting by Value

**The mistake:** Charging the same price to a solo freelancer and a 500-person enterprise.

**The consequence:** You're either too expensive for small customers or leaving massive revenue on the table from large customers. One price can't serve both segments.

**The fix:**
- Identify the value metric that scales with customer size (seats, usage, revenue, features)
- Small customers should pay less, large customers should pay more — because they get more value
- Enterprise pricing should be 10-50x your smallest plan, not 2-3x
- Per-seat pricing is the simplest way to scale with team size

---

## Mistake 12: Underestimating Willingness to Pay

**The mistake:** Assuming customers won't pay more than $X because that's what competitors charge or what feels reasonable to you.

**The consequence:** You price based on your own financial sensibilities instead of the customer's value perception. A tool that saves a business 10 hours/week is worth far more than $20/month.

**The fix:**
- Calculate the ROI your product delivers (time saved × hourly rate, revenue generated, cost avoided)
- Price at 10-20% of the value delivered — this feels like a bargain to the customer
- B2B customers evaluate tools on ROI, not absolute cost
- Run a Van Westendorp price sensitivity survey with 50+ potential customers

---

## Mistake 13: Changing Prices Without Grandfathering

**The mistake:** Raising prices on existing customers without warning or option to keep their current plan.

**The consequence:** Mass churn. Social media backlash. Trust destruction. Customers who feel trapped will leave as soon as they can.

**The fix:**
- Always grandfather existing customers at their current price (or offer extended notice)
- New pricing applies to new customers only
- If you must change existing customer pricing, give 6-12 months notice and justify the change with new value
- Offer annual lock-in as a way for existing customers to secure current pricing

---

## Pricing Strategy Quick Reference

| Situation | Recommended Approach |
|-----------|---------------------|
| New product, unknown market | Start higher, offer early adopter discount |
| Entering competitive market | Match mid-range, differentiate on value |
| Developer tool | Generous free tier, paid for teams/scale |
| B2B SaaS | Per-seat pricing, 3 tiers + enterprise |
| Consumer app | Freemium or low monthly, annual incentive |
| Marketplace | Transaction fee (5-15%), keep it simple |
| One-time purchase | Price at 3-6 months of subscription equivalent |

---

## The Pricing Review Checklist

Run through this every 6 months:

- [ ] Does our pricing reflect current value delivered?
- [ ] Are we losing deals primarily on price? (If not, we might be too cheap)
- [ ] Is our free tier driving conversions or just freeloaders?
- [ ] Can a prospect understand our pricing in under 30 seconds?
- [ ] Do our tiers map to clear customer segments?
- [ ] Have we tested pricing in the last 6 months?
- [ ] Are we grandfathering existing customers on price changes?
- [ ] Does our annual discount make financial sense?
